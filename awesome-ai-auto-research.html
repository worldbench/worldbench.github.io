From Idea to Impact

A fully automated AI system today can generate a research paper every 2.3 hours at $15, yet every leading LLM will fabricate academic results under pressure. This capability-vs-integrity tension defines the current moment in AI-assisted research.

We present the first end-to-end analysis of AI across the complete research lifecycle, organizing eight stages into four epistemological phases: Creation (idea generation, literature review, coding & experiments, figures & tables), Validation (peer review, rebuttal & revision), Communication (paper writing), and Dissemination (posters, slides, video, social media) — three stages of which are entirely absent from all prior work.

Drawing on over 200 papers published since 2023, we surface six cross-cutting insights from current AI auto-research systems and identify a shared three-layer architecture that the most successful systems converge on.

Key Insights

Six Cross-Cutting Findings

Drawing on over 200 papers, we surface six meta-level insights that emerge across all stages of the AI auto-research pipeline, supported by quantitative evidence from controlled experiments involving hundreds of researchers.

5.38 → 3.41
The Ideation-Execution Gap

LLM-generated ideas appear novel but collapse upon execution. Quality scores drop from 5.38 to 3.41 after implementation by human researchers.

37.3%
The Code Translation Ceiling

AI struggles to translate research papers into working code, plateauing near 37% on novel algorithmic tasks despite exceeding 80% on standard benchmarks.

5.36 / 5.39
The Quality Ceiling

The best end-to-end systems (CycleResearcher: 5.36) generate papers that fall just below the acceptance threshold (5.39) of major venues.

$15 – $1K
Cost-Quality Decoupling

Cost and quality decouple exponentially beyond a certain compute budget, from $15/paper (AI Scientist) to $1,000/paper (FARS).

>1/5
AI Contamination Rate

Over a fifth of CS abstracts already carry detectable AI-modified content, raising integrity concerns across the field.

3-Layer
Convergent Architecture

The most successful systems converge on a shared three-layer architecture: planning, execution, and verification.
